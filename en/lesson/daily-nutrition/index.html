What to eat as a runner — day to day, not just race day

Most running nutrition advice focuses on race day. But what you eat Monday to Saturday determines how well you train, recover, and stay injury-free.

Key idea: Match your carbohydrate intake to your training load. Eat more on hard days, less on easy days. Protein is not optional — it is the raw material for tendon repair.

Carbohydrate periodisation is the single most impactful daily nutrition habit for runners. On hard or long sessions, you need glycogen Lesson 3 — eat a carbohydrate-rich meal 2–3 hours before and replenish within 30–60 minutes after. On easy Z2 days, deliberately keeping carbohydrate intake moderate trains the body to access fat more efficiently. This is not a fad — it is how elite runners have trained for decades.

Protein is chronically underestimated by recreational runners. The target is 1.6–2.0 g per kg of body weight per day. Distribute it across meals: muscle protein synthesis peaks at around 30 g per meal and does not increase with larger doses. Protein's primary role for runners is not muscle bulk — it is tendon collagen production and connective tissue repair.

The most important timing window is the 30–60 minutes after a hard or long session: aim for 1 g/kg of carbohydrate and 25–30 g of protein. This combination restores glycogen and triggers repair simultaneously.

Two deficiencies that disproportionately affect runners Iron: foot strike haemolysis (red blood cells destroyed by impact) causes runners to lose iron faster than most people. Low iron → low ferritin → oxygen-carrying capacity drops → unexplained fatigue and slower paces. Red meat, legumes and shellfish are the best sources; pair with vitamin C to improve absorption. Get ferritin (not just haemoglobin) tested annually.

Magnesium: involved in over 300 enzyme reactions including glycogen metabolism and muscle relaxation. Deficiency causes cramping, poor sleep quality and elevated resting heart rate — all of which degrade training. Many runners are borderline deficient. Dark leafy greens, nuts, seeds and dark chocolate are good sources.

Fat adaptation and Zone 2: running the majority of your easy sessions at a truly low intensity — where fat provides most of the fuel — gradually improves your fat oxidation capacity. This means you can run longer before relying on glycogen, and your race-day gel strategy buys more time. This is built over months of consistent Z2 work, not from a few fasted runs.

Hydration beyond race day: arrive at every session already hydrated. A rule of thumb: your urine should be pale yellow, not clear (over-hydration) or dark (under-hydration). Electrolytes matter most in sessions over 60 minutes or in heat; for shorter easy runs, plain water is sufficient.

Common myth "Runners need lots of carbs all the time." Only on hard training days. Chronically high carbohydrate intake on easy days suppresses the fat-burning adaptation that makes you a more efficient runner.
The simplest daily habit Add a palm-sized portion of protein to every meal. Most recreational runners eat enough carbohydrates but consistently under-eat protein. This single change has the highest return for tendon health and recovery.

Which deficiency most commonly causes unexplained fatigue and slowing paces in runners, often missed by standard blood tests?
Correct — low ferritin. Standard blood tests often check haemoglobin, which stays normal until iron stores are severely depleted. Ferritin (the storage form of iron) can drop into a performance-limiting range long before anaemia develops. Ask specifically for a ferritin test.
