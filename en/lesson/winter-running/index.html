Winter running

Cold, ice and short days are no reason to stop. With the right clothing and technique you can run even at −20°C.

Key idea: Cold air is safe for running. The real problems are over-warm clothing, slippery surfaces, and dehydration — which in the cold often goes unnoticed.

Cold air is not dangerous for your lungs — even at −20°C, the air warms to body temperature by the time it reaches the bronchi. The real challenges of winter running are: slippery surfaces, over-warm clothing that causes heavy sweating (and subsequent chilling), and thirst — felt less strongly in the cold even though fluid needs remain.

Clothing works in layers like an onion: the first layer (against the skin) — a moisture-wicking synthetic fabric, not cotton (cotton absorbs sweat and chills you). The second — an insulating fleece or padded jacket. The third (at −5°C and below) — a wind- and rain-resistant shell.

Running on ice or snow requires adjusted technique: shorter strides, a lower centre of mass, and the foot landing directly under the body rather than in front of it. Running speed will naturally drop 10–20% — this is normal and correct.

In the cold, muscles and tendons warm up more slowly. Warm-up becomes even more important: 5–10 minutes of walking or very slow running before increasing intensity is strongly recommended. Lesson 7: Technique and injuries

Icy surfaces Running spikes or Yaktrax-type anti-slip covers significantly reduce the risk of slipping. If you don't have them, choose snow or mud trails rather than smooth ice. On glazed asphalt, run with a slightly wider stance, lower centre of mass and more controlled steps.
Cold-weather risks Frostbite — at −10°C and below, especially in wind. Protect your ears, fingers and face. Hypothermia — the main danger when you stop, start sweating and cool down rapidly — always have quick access to a warm shelter. Dehydration — thirst signals are weaker in the cold, but you still sweat.
Why should the first layer of winter running clothing not be cotton?
Yes. Cotton retains moisture, and the moment you stop, wet clothing chills the body very quickly. Synthetic fabrics (polypropylene, Merino wool) wick moisture away from the skin.
