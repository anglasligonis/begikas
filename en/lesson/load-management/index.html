Lesson 17 of 22

Load management — how to progress without getting injured

Most beginner injuries come not from weakness but from progressing too quickly. The heart adapts in weeks — tendons take months.

Key idea: The greatest injury risk comes not when load is high, but when one week's load significantly exceeds the average of the previous weeks.

There is a paradox that almost every running enthusiast encounters. After a few weeks of training you feel strong, running gets easier, you want more. Heart rate on easy runs drops, reserves appear. And then the temptation rises naturally: why not an extra kilometre? Why not an extra session?

At this point the heart and lungs agree — they truly have adapted. But tendons, ligaments and bones run on a different biological clock. Muscle adaptation happens in 2–4 weeks. Tendon collagen renewal takes 3–6 months Lesson 10: Strength training. Bone mineral density changes even more slowly. This means that when you feel ready for more — your connective tissues are not yet.

More important than absolute load is its dynamics. Research analysing the causes of running injuries found that the greatest risk arises not when load is high, but when one week's load substantially exceeds the average of previous weeks. In practice this means: after a big week, a recovery week with lower volume and lower intensity is necessary. After injury or illness you cannot immediately return to the same volume. Even after just two weeks off, tissues begin reverting to a lower load tolerance.

Three high-risk situations Injury risk is highest in these situations: the first 8 weeks of running (tissues not yet adapted), the build-up to a first half marathon or marathon (temptation to ramp volume quickly), and returning after a longer break. In all three cases — be more conservative than seems necessary.
Weekly check A simple weekly test: is this week's total mileage no more than 10% higher than the average of the past 4 weeks? If yes, you're in a safe zone. If it's more, consider holding progress back for a week.
One at a time Sports medicine specialists suggest a simple rule: increase either volume or intensity — not both in the same week. This rule reduces injury risk at very little cost to overall progress.

Why do many injuries occur even when training feels easy?
Exactly. The cardiovascular system adapts in weeks. Tendon collagen takes months. This means the feeling of aerobic readiness always outpaces tissue readiness — which is why conservative progression matters even when everything feels easy.
