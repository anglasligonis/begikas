Running economy — why some runners find it easier

Two runners, the same pace, the same VO₂max, and completely different levels of fatigue. That's running economy.

Key idea: Running economy is the energy cost of a given pace. A better runner is not necessarily stronger, more often they are simply more efficient.

Andrew and Tom run together. Same pace, same distance. After the run, Andrew feels fine, Tom is considerably more tired. Their heart rates during the run differed by ten beats. Both healthy, both trained. The difference, running economy.

Running economy is measured as oxygen cost at a given pace. The less oxygen consumed at a certain pace, the better the economy. For athletes this matters just as much as VO₂max Lesson 13: VO₂max, in many cases even more, because it can be trained far more than genetically determined VO₂max.

Economy is shaped by several factors together. Technique, how the stride transfers impact energy to the ground, how much energy is wasted on lateral sway and trunk rotation Lesson 7: Technique. Tendon elasticity, especially the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia's ability to store and return elastic energy. Muscle coordination, how little the antagonist muscles activate when they shouldn't. And experience, people who have run for many years show better economy even when VO₂max no longer improves.

This is one of physiology's comforting truths: economy improves simply by running a lot and consistently. A beginner after their first year of running expends 10–15% less energy at the same pace than at the start, even without any specific technical drills. The body learns to optimise movement on its own. Strength training accelerates this process, especially eccentric exercises that strengthen tendons. Lesson 10: Strength training

How to improve economy The best tools for developing running economy: consistent mileage, strength training (especially single-leg exercises), running drills, and increasing cadence to 170–180 SPM. None of them require a laboratory or expensive equipment.
Common myth "Lighter runners are always more economical." Not always. Body weight affects economy, but muscle composition, tendon properties and technique are often more important. Some heavier runners are more economical than lighter ones, thanks to better elasticity and coordination.
Home test Run 10 minutes at the same pace after 3 months of consistent training. If your heart rate is lower than it was before, your economy is improving. No laboratory needed. It's the simplest and cheapest measure of progress.

Running economy primarily improves through:
Exactly. Economy is not a single metric. It is the sum of many factors. Consistent running teaches the body to move more efficiently. Strength work strengthens tendons, which return elastic energy. VO₂max has no direct effect on economy.
