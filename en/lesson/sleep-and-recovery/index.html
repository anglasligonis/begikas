Lesson 21 of 22

Sleep, recovery and when adaptation actually happens

The best training session counts for nothing if you don't recover from it. Sleep is not rest. It is where fitness is built.

Key idea: Training is the stimulus. Sleep is the adaptation. Skipping sleep is like planting seeds and then forgetting to water them.

When you run, you create micro-damage in muscle fibres and tendons. This is intentional, the body repairs them stronger. But the repair happens during sleep, not during the run. In the first two hours of deep sleep, the pituitary gland releases the majority of your daily growth hormone (HGH), the key signal for protein synthesis and tendon collagen remodelling.

REM sleep (later in the night) consolidates motor patterns learned during the session. A runner who practises landing under the body Lesson 7 but sleeps only 5 hours retains less of that neuromuscular learning than one who sleeps 8.

The target for runners is 7–9 hours per night. Quantity matters, but so does quality, a fragmented 8 hours produces less slow-wave sleep than a solid 7.

Morning resting HR — the simplest recovery signal Measure your pulse for 60 seconds before getting out of bed. Track it for 2–3 weeks to establish your personal baseline. If it is 5–7 bpm above your baseline, your body is still under stress from the previous session. That is not the day for a hard workout.

Active vs passive recovery is a meaningful distinction. Passive recovery is doing nothing. Active recovery is very easy movement, a 20–30 minute Z1 walk or gentle jog. At low intensity, blood flow increases to tendons and muscles without adding training load, which speeds clearance of inflammatory byproducts. Most runners benefit more from active recovery than complete rest on their "off" days.

Signs you are under-recovering: persistent muscle soreness beyond 48 hours, motivation loss, declining pace at the same heart rate over several weeks Lesson 20, irritability, and poor sleep despite physical fatigue. One or two of these occasionally is normal. Multiple together for more than a week signals overreaching.

Practical habits Consistent sleep and wake times matter more than total hours. The body's hormonal recovery schedule is tied to circadian rhythm, sleeping 7 hours at consistent times is more restorative than 8 hours at irregular ones. Avoid intense runs within 3 hours of bedtime: elevated core temperature and cortisol from hard sessions delay sleep onset.
Common myth "I can catch up on sleep at the weekend." Partial sleep debt accumulates across the week and impairs recovery even if you sleep in on Saturday. Consistency beats compensation.
