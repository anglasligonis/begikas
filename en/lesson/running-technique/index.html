Running technique and injury prevention

Injuries are the main reason beginners give up running. Most of them can be prevented by changing a few simple things.

Key idea: Increase your cadence to 170–180 SPM. It's one of the simplest ways to reduce knee load and injury risk.

Running technique doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be safe and efficient. There's no single correct running style: elite runners look very different from each other. But there are a few common mistakes that cause most beginner injuries.

Overstriding, the most common mistake. It happens when the foot lands far in front of the body's centre of mass and acts as a brake. Every such stride sends impact force straight to the knee joint. The fix: shorten your stride and increase your cadence (steps per minute) to 170–180 SPM.

Arm swing crossing the midline, when your arms swing inward rather than straight forward and back, the body compensates by rotating. This wastes energy and loads the lower back muscles. Keep your elbows at ~90°, wrists relaxed, arm swing should go straight forward.

Heavy heel strike, not everyone condemns a heel landing, but if you can clearly hear the impact as your foot lands, your stride is probably too long. Try to land your foot under your body, not in front of it.

Trunk upright, lean slight, from the ankles, ~5–10° forward, lets gravity help you move forward. Those who stand completely upright often brake themselves with every step.

Most common beginner injuries IT band syndrome (ITBS), pain on the outside of the knee, often from increasing mileage too quickly. Shin splints, pain along the shinbone, often from hard surfaces and rapid volume increases. Plantar fasciitis, heel pain, often from weak foot muscles and poor shoes. For all three: reduce volume, strengthen weak areas, switch to a softer surface. Strength training helps prevent many of these injuries, see Lesson 10: Strength training.

Practical tip To increase cadence: play music at 170–180 BPM and try to match your footfall to the beat. Or run with a metronome app. Increase by 5 SPM per week, a sudden change can cause shin pain.
Warm-up and cool-down Before running: 5–10 min easy walking or slow jogging + dynamic exercises (leg swings, squats). This prepares muscles and joints and reduces injury risk. After running: 5 min slow walking to let your heart rate drop gradually. Save static stretching for later. It's unnecessary before a run and can even be counterproductive.
About shoes For beginners, the most important thing is comfortable, correctly sized running shoes (leave ~1 cm of space at the toes). You don't need to buy the most expensive or carbon-plated shoes. It's best to visit a specialist running store where staff can help choose based on your foot shape and running style. Replace shoes approximately every 600–800 km.
What is the simplest way to reduce impact force on the knee?
Correct. A higher cadence (170–180 SPM) with a shorter stride means your foot lands under your body, not in front of it, reducing braking force and knee load. Increase cadence gradually, ~5 SPM per week.
