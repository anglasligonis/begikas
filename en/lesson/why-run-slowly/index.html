Why do beginners need to run slowly?

Many beginners try to run too fast from day one. Understandable, but physiologically it's a mistake. Here's why.

Key idea: 80% of your running should be easy enough that you can hold a conversation. That's not laziness. It's a sports-science-backed principle.

Your muscles have two types of fibres: slow-twitch (Type I) and fast-twitch (Type II). Slow fibres are efficient. They use oxygen and fat as fuel and can work for hours. Fast fibres are powerful, but fatigue quickly.

When you run easily, almost only slow fibres work. When you speed up, the body recruits fast-twitch fibres and lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared Lesson 4: Lactate. Running becomes hard not because you're weak, but because more and more work falls on the fast-twitch fibres.

Speed is built through slow training. 80% of your sessions should be easy, easy enough to hold a conversation. That's not laziness; it's a principle confirmed by sports science.

The key point Talk Test: if you can chat in full sentences while running, your pace is right. If only short replies. You're going too fast.

Common myth "No pain, no gain." Not true. Easy running builds your capillary network, trains your muscles to burn fat, and strengthens tendons. It's the foundation on which all speed is built.
For beginners If continuous running is still difficult, use the run-walk method: alternate 1–2 minutes of running with 1 minute of walking. This lets you move for longer, reduces tendon stress, and gradually builds endurance. Many successful runners started exactly this way.

Where do you actually start? Here is a simple plan for your first four weeks, built on the run-walk method. Run every interval at an easy, conversational pace: if you can no longer talk, slow down or walk. Train 3 times a week and leave at least one rest day between sessions.

Week 1: getting used to it
3 × per week, about 25 min
Walk 5 min to warm up, then 8 × (1 min easy running + 2 min walking). No speed at all. The goal is simply to finish.
Week 2: longer intervals
3 × per week, about 26 min
Walk 5 min, then 6 × (2 min running + 1.5 min walking). Pay attention to your calves and knees: mild muscle fatigue is normal, sharp pain is not.
Week 3: more running than walking
3 × per week, about 27 min
Walk 5 min, then 4 × (4 min running + 1.5 min walking). If it feels too hard, repeat week 2. That is not falling behind, that is smart training.
Week 4: almost continuous
3 × per week, about 29 min
Walk 5 min, then 3 × (7 min running + 1 min walking). After this week, most people can run 20 minutes without stopping.

Why only 3 times a week? Your body gets stronger not during the workout but while resting after it. This mechanism is called supercompensation Lesson 8: How to improve. On top of that, tendons and joints adapt to impact more slowly than your heart and muscles do, so for a beginner rest days are not a luxury. They are part of the plan.

The most common beginner mistakes. Almost all of them come from too much enthusiasm:

Running too fast. Mistake number one. If you finish a run completely drained, you ran too fast. An easy day should leave you feeling like you had more in the tank.

Increasing volume too quickly. Grow your weekly volume by no more than ~10%. More on this in Lesson 17: Load management.

Ignoring pain signals. Muscle soreness fades within a day or two. Sharp or localised pain, or pain you feel even while walking, is a different story Lesson 18: Pain or injury.

Comparing yourself to others. Your easy pace depends on your physiology, not on your Strava friends. A year of consistent training from now, your current pace will look amusingly slow, but only if you reach that year without injury.

What share of training should be easy, conversational runs?
Yes, about 80% of training should be easy. This builds your aerobic base and allows the body to recover between harder sessions.
