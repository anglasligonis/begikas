Why do beginners need to run slowly?

Many beginners try to run too fast from day one. Understandable — but physiologically it's a mistake. Here's why.

Key idea: 80% of your running should be easy enough that you can hold a conversation. That's not laziness — it's a sports-science-backed principle.

Your muscles have two types of fibres: slow-twitch (Type I) and fast-twitch (Type II). Slow fibres are efficient — they use oxygen and fat as fuel and can work for hours. Fast fibres are powerful, but fatigue quickly.

When you run easily, almost only slow fibres work. When you speed up, the body recruits fast-twitch fibres and lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared Lesson 4: Lactate. Running becomes hard not because you're weak, but because more and more work falls on the fast-twitch fibres.

Speed is built through slow training. 80% of your sessions should be easy — easy enough to hold a conversation. That's not laziness; it's a principle confirmed by sports science.

The key point Talk Test: if you can chat in full sentences while running, your pace is right. If only short replies — you're going too fast.

Common myth "No pain, no gain." Not true. Easy running builds your capillary network, trains your muscles to burn fat, and strengthens tendons. It's the foundation on which all speed is built.
For beginners If continuous running is still difficult, use the run-walk method: alternate 1–2 minutes of running with 1 minute of walking. This lets you move for longer, reduces tendon stress, and gradually builds endurance. Many successful runners started exactly this way.
What share of training should be easy, conversational runs?
Yes — about 80% of training should be easy. This builds your aerobic base and allows the body to recover between harder sessions.
