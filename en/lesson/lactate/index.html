Lactate — not the enemy, but a signal

Lactate has a bad reputation. "Lactic acid will burn your muscles!" In reality, that's not quite true. Here's what actually happens.

Key idea: Lactate is not the enemy — it's a fuel. The burning sensation is caused by hydrogen ions, not lactate. The lactate threshold rises with training.

When a muscle is short of oxygen, it breaks down glucose and produces lactate. This is normal and happens constantly. The problem lies not in the lactate itself, but in the hydrogen ions (H⁺) produced alongside it, which acidify the muscle environment. That's what causes the burning sensation and fatigue.

Lactate is itself an energy source — the heart burns it, and other muscles take it up and use it. It only accumulates when it's produced faster than it can be cleared.

The lactate threshold — the intensity (see Lesson 2: Zones) beyond which lactate begins to accumulate exponentially. An experienced runner reaches this threshold much later (at a higher pace) than an untrained one. The goal of training is to push this threshold as high as possible.

What does "threshold HR" mean? It's the point — as a percentage of your maximum heart rate — beyond which lactate accumulates faster than it's cleared. For example, if your MaxHR is 190 and your threshold is 75%, that means around ~143 BPM running will become noticeably harder. This threshold rises with training — so as you move the upper slider, you'll see the curve shift to the right.

Common myth "Next-day muscle soreness is caused by lactic acid." Not true. Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) comes from micro-tears in the muscle fibres — not from lactate. Lactate clears from the blood within a few hours of finishing a run.
What actually causes the burning sensation in your muscles?
Exactly. Lactate is actually a useful fuel. The burning is caused by the hydrogen ions produced alongside it, which lower muscle pH. Next-day soreness (DOMS) is micro-tears — not lactate.
