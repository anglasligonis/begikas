Lesson 8 of 22

How does a runner actually improve?

Speed increases not through willpower, but through specific biological adaptations. These only happen when you train correctly.

Key idea: Your body improves during recovery, not during the run. Sleep 7–9 hours and increase your weekly volume by no more than 10% per week.

Every time you run, you put your body under stress. Afterwards, as it recovers, the body becomes slightly stronger — this is called supercompensation. But only when the stress was appropriate and the rest sufficient.

Specific adaptations that make you faster:

Heart: the volume of the left ventricle increases — with each beat the heart pumps more blood. After months of training, resting heart rate drops.

Muscles: mitochondria (the cell's energy factories) multiply, the capillary network becomes denser, and muscles learn to use more oxygen per minute.

Tendons and bones: strengthen more slowly than muscles — this is the most common cause of injury in beginners who increase volume too quickly.

The most important principle — the 10% rule: don't increase weekly mileage by more than 10%. It's not an absolute rule: for some, especially beginners, 5% is safer. Muscles adapt in 2–3 weeks. Bones and tendons — in 3–6 months. If your body sends warning signals earlier, listen to it, not the calendar.

Sleep and rest — not a luxury, but part of training. Adaptation happens not during the run, but during sleep, when the body repairs muscle fibres, produces growth hormone, and consolidates neuromotor skills. Fewer than 7 hours of sleep consistently slows progress and increases injury risk.

Beginner plan (summary) 80% of sessions — easy (you can talk). 15% — moderate (tempo). 5% — intense (intervals). Increase mileage no faster than ~10% per week. After every hard session — an easy day or rest. Sleep 7–9 hrs.

One of the most important tips The best training session is the one you can repeat next week. Healthy, consistent running always beats heroic but injury-causing running.
When does physical adaptation (getting stronger) actually happen?
Yes. Training is only the stimulus — the actual strengthening (supercompensation) happens during recovery, especially sleep. That's why rest and 7–9 hrs of sleep are just as important a part of training as running itself.
