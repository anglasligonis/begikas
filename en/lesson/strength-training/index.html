Lesson 10 of 22

Strength training for runners

Running alone doesn't protect against injuries. Strength work is not a supplementary activity. It's an essential part of a running programme.

Key idea: 2 strength sessions per week reduce injury risk, improve running economy and let you run faster, even without adding kilometres.

Running is a single-leg movement, with every stride, the entire body weight falls on one foot. This demands considerable hip, gluteal and core stability strength. When these muscles are too weak, impact force is redistributed to the knees, shins and ankles, where most running injuries begin.

Runners don't aim for muscle mass with strength training. The goal is stability, eccentric strength and tendon resilience. Research shows that a 6–8 week strength programme improves running economy (less energy for the same pace) by an average of 2–8%.

Most importantly: start strength work before pain appears, as prevention, not treatment. Muscles respond in 4–6 weeks, tendons in 3–6 months. Lesson 7: Technique and injuries

How to integrate Do strength sessions after an easy run or on separate days, not before a hard interval session. 2× per week, 20–30 min. Start with bodyweight exercises, weights are only needed once 3×12 reps feel easy.
Common mistake Beginners often start strength training only when they're already in pain. By then the tendon or joint is already damaged. Better: 15 min of calf and hip exercises 2× per week from the very first week of running.
Why does strength training improve running economy?
Exactly. Strength work improves tendon elasticity and the function of stabilising muscles, meaning less energy is wasted on compensatory movements and the body transfers force to the ground more efficiently.
