Lesson 3 of 22

How does the body fuel running?

Your body has two main fuel sources. Which one is used depends on your pace, and this determines everything from weight loss to marathon finish times.

Key idea: Fat is a nearly inexhaustible source, but slow. Glycogen is fast, but limited. Slow training teaches the body to conserve glycogen.

Fat is a nearly inexhaustible energy source. Even a lean 70 kg person carries ~80,000–100,000 kcal in body fat. But there's an important limit: even a well-trained athlete's body can burn fat no faster than ~0.5–1 g per minute. At high intensity, fat delivers energy too slowly, glycogen becomes unavoidable.

Glycogen, sugar stored in muscles and the liver, is more quickly available, but limited: roughly 1,500–2,000 kcal (enough for ~90 minutes of intense running). When it runs out, the body experiences what marathon runners call the wall, a sudden onset of fatigue around the 30–35 km mark.

By training slowly, you teach the body to burn fat more efficiently and conserve glycogen for longer distances. This is one of the most important physiological adaptations, more in Lesson 8: How to improve.

The marathon wall Around the 32–35 km mark, many marathon runners hit a sudden slowdown. The cause: depleted glycogen. The fix: train slowly for longer (building fat efficiency) and take energy gels from the 45-minute mark.
Nutrition around training Before (1–3 hrs): an easily digestible carbohydrate source (banana, oats, bread). Avoid heavy, fatty foods. After a longer or hard session (within ~1 hr): carbohydrates to replenish glycogen and protein for muscle recovery (e.g. cottage cheese with fruit, yogurt, a sandwich). After an easy short run, no special nutrition is needed, a normal meal is fine.
Why does the marathon wall occur?
Yes. Glycogen stores are limited (~90 min of intense running). When they run out, the body is forced to rely on slow fat oxidation, causing the sudden slowdown. The fix: train fat burning and consume carbohydrates during the run.
