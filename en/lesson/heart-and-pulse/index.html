Heart, pulse and training zones

Pulse rate is the simplest indicator of training intensity. But how do you interpret it correctly?

Key idea: Training zones help you run right. 80% of running should be Z1–Z2. Avoid the so-called grey Z3 zone: it's neither easy enough for adaptation nor hard enough for progress.

Your heart is a pump. Every time a muscle needs more oxygen, it beats faster. Maximum heart rate (MaxHR) is the highest rate your heart can reach. It's most accurately measured in a lab, but there's a simple approximation: 220 − age.

Training zones are defined by percentages of MaxHR. The two most important ones: Zone 2 (60–70% MaxHR), easy, conversational pace where aerobic adaptation happens; and Zone 4 (85–95% MaxHR), the lactate threshold Lesson 4: Lactate, where running becomes truly hard.

A common beginner mistake is spending most training in Zone 3 (70–85%): it's too hard for easy adaptation and too easy for a meaningful training stimulus. This is known as the "grey zone trap".

How to calculate If you're 35: MaxHR ≈ 220 − 35 = 185 BPM. Zone 2 = 111–130 BPM. Zone 4 = 157–176 BPM. Important: the formula has a ±10–12 BPM margin of error and can be significantly off for some people. If your monitor shows Z2 but running feels hard, trust how you feel, not the numbers.

Practical tip If you don't have a heart-rate monitor, use the talk test. Easy (Z2): you can talk freely. Moderate (Z3): short sentences only. Hard (Z4): single-word answers. Very hard (Z5): can't speak at all.
What is the "grey zone trap"?
Correct. Zone 3 (70–85% MaxHR) is a "neither here nor there" zone, too hard to be truly easy, and too easy to provide a meaningful training stimulus. Better to run either truly easy (Z2) or truly hard (Z4).
