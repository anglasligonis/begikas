Lesson 6 of 22

Heat, sweating and hydration

Running slows in summer. That's a normal physiological response. Here's why, and what to do about it.

Key idea: Above 25°C, slow down and drink every 15 minutes. Even 2% dehydration significantly reduces performance.

Running generates a lot of heat. Muscles are only about 25% efficient, and the remaining 75% becomes heat. It must be removed to keep body temperature from straying too far from 37°C.

The body does this mainly through sweating. But to do so, blood is redirected to the skin, the same blood that muscles would otherwise need. On a hot day, the heart must serve two demands at once, so heart rate rises even at the same pace. This is known as cardiac drift.

That's why, at the same pace, heart rate on a hot day can be 10–20 BPM higher than on a cool one. It means that on a hot day you need to slow down to stay in the same training zone.

Even 2% dehydration (1.4 kg for a 70 kg person) significantly reduces aerobic capacity. Drink before you run; while running, don't wait until you feel thirsty.

Practical rule Above 25°C, slow down by roughly 6–8% for every 5°C above 15°C. On a 30°C day, run about 10–15% slower than in cool conditions. Drink ~150–200 ml every 15 minutes, even if you don't feel thirsty.
Why do you need to slow down on a hot day?
Yes. The heart must supply both muscles and skin (for cooling), so the same pace requires a higher heart rate. To stay in the same training zone, you need to slow down.
