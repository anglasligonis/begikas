Lesson 16 of 22

Pace distribution — the art of not starting too fast

Most amateur runners ruin their race in the first few kilometres. Not from weakness, from adrenaline and the wrong strategy.

Key idea: A good result usually starts with patience in the first few kilometres. A negative split, second half faster than the first, is the most effective strategy for most distances.

A split is the time for each kilometre or half of a race. If you run a 10K in 50 minutes and cover the first 5 km in 24 min and the second in 26 min, your split is positive (first half faster). If reversed. It's negative. Watches and race results always list splits, so it's worth knowing what they mean.

"The first five kilometres felt great, then everything fell apart." This is not a sign of weakness. It is a physiologically predictable consequence of an adrenaline- and enthusiasm-fuelled start that went out too hard.

The race start is a psychologically hostile moment for choosing the right pace. The environment is full of stimuli: crowds, music, other runners streaming past. You feel fresh because you tapered before the race. The first kilometre almost always feels too easy, even when you're already going too fast. Energy expenditure at that point doesn't yet reflect the true load, because the body is still ramping up to a stable level of aerobic energy production.

A negative split means running the first half slightly slower and the second slightly faster. Why does it work physiologically? Lactate accumulates exponentially with speed Lesson 4: Lactate. Even a small reduction in early pace significantly limits lactate build-up and lets you run the second half with fresh legs. "Banking time" in the first half is an illusion, the lactate accumulated through early enthusiasm costs far more time in the second half than was ever saved.

The practical recommendation: run the first two kilometres 5–10 seconds per km slower than target pace. If you're aiming for 5:30 min/km, start at 5:35–5:40. It should feel too easy. That's exactly right. Around 30–40% of the way through, begin moving towards your target pace.

Strategy by distance A 5K can be run at near-even pace from the start because the distance is short. In a half marathon or marathon a negative split pays the biggest dividend, the longer the distance, the more costly going out too hard.
If you've already started too fast Don't try to slow abruptly. It is better to ease off gradually by 5–10 seconds per km than to brake suddenly and then try to accelerate again. The priority is to keep breathing rhythmically and maintain an even stride.
Watch alert Before the race set a maximum pace for the first kilometre, a pace you will not exceed under any circumstances, and programme it as a watch alert. When the watch beeps that you're going too fast, that's a message from your calmer pre-race self to your adrenaline-flooded race self.

Why does "banking time" in the first half of a race usually backfire?
Exactly. Lactate accumulates exponentially with speed. Every extra second faster than planned demands disproportionate energy. In the second half, "repaying" that debt costs several times more time than was gained at the start.
