Fatigue — why your body tells you to slow down

Fatigue is not just a muscle problem. It is the whole body's protective system — operating at both the central and peripheral level.

Key idea: The brain starts applying the brakes before true muscle exhaustion. Understanding this lets you manage effort more intelligently — and recognise real overtraining in time.

After running for a long time or at high intensity, a moment arrives when your legs feel heavy, breathing becomes laboured, and the mind wants to stop. Most people call this fatigue and assume the reason is simple — the muscles got tired. But fatigue is a far more complex phenomenon.

Scientists distinguish two types of fatigue: peripheral and central. Peripheral fatigue occurs directly in the muscles and surrounding tissues. During running, glycogen is depleted Lesson 3: Energy, metabolites accumulate, and the muscles' ability to contract diminishes. This fatigue is local — felt in specific muscles.

Central fatigue operates at the level of the brain and nervous system. Before the peripheral muscles reach their true limit of exhaustion, the brain begins sending braking signals. This is described by the central governor theory — the brain continuously monitors the body's state and, anticipating possible danger, reduces muscle activation. This is why approaching the finish line unexpectedly produces a burst of energy — the brain, sensing the end is near, "releases" the protective brakes.

Acute fatigue (what you feel after a single hard session) usually resolves within 24–72 hours. Chronic fatigue is more serious. When the body doesn't get enough rest over several weeks, overtraining sets in. It is recognisable by several signs: morning heart rate higher than usual, easy running feels hard, no desire to train, and sleep doesn't clear the tiredness. If these signs persist for more than a week, a genuine rest week is needed — not just a recovery day.

Overtraining syndrome Overtraining syndrome is a serious condition that takes weeks or even months to recover from. It is caused by sustained load increases without sufficient rest. Unlike ordinary fatigue, overtraining does not resolve after a few rest days — and more training doesn't fix it, it only makes it worse.
Morning heart rate method Measuring your morning heart rate is one of the cheapest and most reliable indicators of chronic fatigue. Take your heart rate at the same time each morning while lying still after waking. If it is 7–10 beats higher than usual for three consecutive days, your body is sending a recovery signal.
Fatigue during races The feeling of fatigue during a race doesn't necessarily mean your muscles are exhausted. It is often a protective brain signal. A trained runner learns to distinguish when that signal represents real danger and when it can be safely ignored — something that develops with experience.

What does "central fatigue" mean?
Exactly. Central fatigue is the nervous system's protective mechanism. The brain continuously monitors the body's condition and reduces muscle activation before true exhaustion is reached. This is one reason why runners almost always find a reserve of energy near the finish line.
