Training systems

The world's best runners train very differently. What actually works — and what can you borrow from them?

Key idea: All systems share one trait: 70–80% of training is easy. The methodologies differ; the principle does not.

There is no single "correct" training system. But there are several well-researched systems used by elite runners, grounded in solid physiological logic. Each has its strengths, limitations, and the contexts where it works best.

It's important to understand: even the best training systems are principles, not recipes. A beginner doesn't need to copy Jakob Ingebrigtsen's training programme — but they can understand why it works and borrow the core logic.

Norwegian double-threshold system
Gjert Ingebrigtsen · Jakob Ingebrigtsen, Filip Ingebrigtsen, Gustav Iden
High volume Not for beginners 5K–marathon Coach supervision required
Principle: two threshold sessions per day — morning and evening — with easy running in between. Intensity is precisely controlled via lactate tests: each threshold session ends at ~2–2.5 mmol/L, never exceeding the threshold. High-intensity intervals (VO₂ Max) are barely used.

The logic is simple: enormous threshold volume → the threshold rises → you can run faster before lactate accumulates. The Norwegian method requires an excellent aerobic base and continuous lactate monitoring — without these, it doesn't work.

Kenyan / Ethiopian model — massive volume, run slowly
Eliud Kipchoge, Kenenisa Bekele, Patrick Sang (coach)
160–220 km/week Altitude 2000m+ Marathon / long distances Group running
Principle: incredibly high volume (160–220 km per week), mostly slow, at altitude. In Iten (Kenya, ~2400 m) and Bekoji (Ethiopia, ~3000 m), children run to school and work from an early age — the aerobic base is built over decades before serious competition.

Speed develops not through frequent intense training, but through an extraordinary aerobic base and altitude adaptation. Group running helps maintain pace and motivation even in very easy sessions.

Polarised model (80/20)
Stephen Seiler · Research-backed model
Research-backed Moderate–high volume All distances Suitable for amateurs
A model of elite athletes researched by Stephen Seiler: 80% very easy training, 20% very hard. The middle zone (Z3) is deliberately avoided — that "comfortable-hard" zone where most amateurs spend most of their training is neither easy enough for aerobic base-building nor hard enough for higher-intensity adaptation.

Research with elite swimmers, cyclists and runners consistently shows that the polarised model outperforms traditional "moderate intensity everywhere". Highly suited to amateurs, as it reduces injury risk and allows long-term training without overtraining.

Lydiard system — aerobic base
Arthur Lydiard · New Zealand · 1950s–60s
Historically influential High volume Marathon / half marathon Long base-building
The system developed by Arthur Lydiard: first, high volume (160+ km per week) at a purely aerobic pace — months without intensive intervals. Only then: hill running, intervals, speed work. The system produced several Olympic champions (Peter Snell, Murray Halberg) and fundamentally changed the understanding of running training worldwide.

The core idea: without a solid aerobic base, no speed work will reach its full potential. Lydiard's influence is felt in every modern training system — the polarised model, the Norwegian school and Kenyan practice.

Daniels' formula (VDOT system)
Jack Daniels · "Daniels' Running Formula" (1998)
Structured Moderate volume 5K–marathon Suitable for amateurs
A system developed by Jack Daniels that calculates a runner's physiological capacity (VDOT) from race results and sets 5 precise training zones with specific paces: Easy (E), Marathon (M), Threshold (T), Interval (I) and Repetition (R). Each run type has a clear physiological purpose.

Very popular among amateurs for its specificity — you know exactly what pace to run in every session. The system automatically recalibrates as performance improves. A good choice for those who prefer structure and a clear system.

Universal principle In all five training systems — Norwegian, Kenyan, polarised, Lydiard and Daniels — one trait dominates: the majority of training is easy. Whichever system you choose, the 80/20 principle applies to all.
What single trait is common to all training systems discussed here?
Yes. From Norwegian double-threshold to Kenyan high volume — 70–80% of training is done at an easy, conversational pace everywhere. This is a consistent pattern: the aerobic base is the foundation of every training system.
