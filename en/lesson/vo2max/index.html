VO₂max — how much oxygen your body can use

VO₂max is one of the most important endurance metrics. But what does it actually mean, and is it worth worrying about?

Key idea: VO₂max shows your physiological potential, but says nothing about how well you use it. Two runners with the same VO₂max can perform very differently.

Imagine two cars with identical engines. One travels further on the same amount of fuel, not because the engine is more powerful, but because the fuel is used more efficiently. The same principle applies to runners.

VO₂max is the maximum volume of oxygen in millilitres that the body can consume per minute per kilogram of body weight (ml/kg/min). It is measured in a lab on a treadmill run to exhaustion, or estimated from race results. An average untrained man scores around 35–40 ml/kg/min. An elite marathon runner, 70–85 ml/kg/min.

However, it's important to understand: VO₂max is not the only determinant of speed. It shows how much oxygen your body can theoretically consume, but not how efficiently it uses that oxygen Lesson 14: Running economy, nor at what pace lactate begins to accumulate Lesson 4: Lactate. For these reasons, two runners with identical VO₂max scores can produce very different race results.

VO₂max improves most from high-intensity interval training, sessions run at 90–100% of maximum heart rate. However, for a beginner runner in the first year, VO₂max rises even from consistent easy training, as the heart strengthens, capillary density increases, and muscles learn to use more oxygen. An expensive lab test is not needed at this stage.

The key point If your watch or app shows a "VO₂max estimate". It is only an algorithmically calculated figure based on heart rate and pace. True VO₂max is measured only in a laboratory with an oxygen analyser. A watch reading can differ from the real value by 10–15%.
Common myth "A high VO₂max means a fast runner." Not always. Many elite runners win not because of a record-breaking VO₂max, but because of exceptional running economy or a very high lactate threshold. VO₂max sets the ceiling, how much of that ceiling you reach depends on many other factors.
For beginners For beginners, a VO₂max figure tells you little about day-to-day training. What matters more is consistently maintaining easy pace and noticing how the same pace feels progressively easier over time. That is a practical sign of rising VO₂max, no watch required.

Why can two runners with the same VO₂max produce very different race results?
Exactly. VO₂max only shows physiological oxygen capacity, like engine size. Final race speed is also shaped by running economy, lactate threshold and training experience. All these factors together determine the actual result.
