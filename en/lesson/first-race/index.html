Your first race — how to prepare

The start line for the first time, exciting and daunting. Many beginner mistakes can be avoided simply by knowing what to expect.

Key idea: In your first race, one mistake outweighs all others: going out too fast. Start 10–15% slower than you plan. You can always pick up the pace in the second half.

The race environment, crowds, music, race numbers, triggers an adrenaline surge that pushes you to start too fast. This is a well-known phenomenon and the most common reason runners "blow up" in the second half of the race.

In your first race, the goal is not a personal record but a strong finish with no regrets. Leaving something in reserve lets you enjoy the finishing straight rather than fighting your own legs.

Race-day nutrition should be based on what you already know from training. Race day is not the time to experiment with new foods or gels. Lesson 3: Energy & fuel

After the race, your body deserves rest. Easy running is fine the next day, but intense training should wait 1–2 weeks depending on the distance. After a marathon, full recovery can take up to 4 weeks. Lesson 8: How to improve

Common myth "You need to eat lots of carbs before a race." The principle is right, the scale is wrong. Carb-loading matters only for marathons and ultra distances. For a 5K or 10K, eating normally the day before is completely sufficient.
Runner wisdom In their first race, almost everyone goes out too fast. It's a normal feeling. Set yourself a goal not of speed but of not accelerating in the first kilometre, and you will almost certainly finish better than you expected.
Why is going out too fast in a race dangerous?
Yes. Going out too fast recruits fast-twitch fibres and burns through glycogen early. Lactate accumulates sooner than it should, in the second half your legs feel heavy and pace drops sharply. An even start is always the better strategy.
