Breathing while running: nose or mouth?

Breathing is the primary way to supply muscles with oxygen. How you breathe matters more than most people think.

Key idea: Easy pace — breathe through your nose. Hard pace — breathe through your mouth. Breathing is driven by CO₂, not by a lack of oxygen.

Breathing is not controlled by a lack of oxygen. It's controlled by CO₂ levels in the blood. When CO₂ builds up, the brain signals you to inhale. That's why, once you learn to tolerate slightly more CO₂, you breathe more calmly and efficiently.

Nasal breathing — slower, but higher quality. The nose warms and humidifies the air, produces nitric oxide (which dilates the pulmonary blood vessels), and encourages diaphragmatic breathing. For easy and moderate pace — it's the best option.

When intensity exceeds ~70% MaxHR, nasal breathing can no longer meet the demand for air. At that point the mouth becomes essential, and that's completely normal. Don't try to breathe through your nose during interval sessions — it hinders rather than helps.

It's important to learn to breathe with the diaphragm (belly breathing), not the chest. This draws more air with each breath.

Practical tip Learn to breathe with your belly: lie down and place a hand on your stomach. When you inhale, your belly should rise — not your chest. Practise this at rest and it will become automatic while running.
