Pain or injury — how to tell the difference

Not every pain requires rest. Not every pain can be ignored. The ability to tell them apart is one of the most important skills a runner can develop.

Key idea: Muscle soreness after a hard session is normal. Pain that increases during a run and is concentrated in one specific spot needs attention.

Running without any discomfort is an unrealistic expectation. Muscle fatigue, parts of the body not previously challenged, first long distances, all of this naturally produces sensations that are easily confused with injury. But not all such sensations are dangerous. Ignored injuries often develop into something more serious, while excessive attention to normal sensations leads to unnecessary breaks.

Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) is that familiar heavy, aching feeling in the legs the day after a hard session. It comes from microscopic muscle fibre tears, which are a normal part of the adaptation process. DOMS is typically felt on both sides symmetrically, appears 12–48 hours after exercise, eases with light movement, and passes within 2–5 days. It is not an injury. It is a natural part of adaptation.

Signs that deserve closer attention are different. Pain that increases during a run, especially if it wasn't present at the start, is a warning signal. Pain concentrated in one precise spot, not diffuse muscle soreness but a point you can locate exactly, requires attention. Limping or an altered running style caused by pain means the body is guarding the sore area by shifting load to other structures. That is no longer discomfort, it is compensation. Pain that does not resolve after a rest day but returns immediately on the next run warrants a consultation with a sports medicine specialist.

A practical rule used by many sports medicine specialists: pain up to 3 out of 10 during a run. You can continue training. 4–5, reduce intensity and volume. 6 or above, stop and rest. If pain doesn't begin to ease within 5–7 rest days, see a specialist Lesson 17: Load management.

Tendon pain Tendon pain (Achilles, patellar tendon, IT band) is often insidious. It starts at just 2–3 out of 10 and seems manageable, but if ignored it can develop into a chronic condition requiring months of treatment. The classic sign: pain "disappears" in the first few minutes of a run, then returns afterwards. This is not a sign of improvement. It is a signal of overload.
Common myth "You need to run through the pain." This advice applies only to DOMS-type muscle soreness, which genuinely eases with light movement. For tendon, bone or joint pain it is harmful advice. Light movement, fine. Running through pain and hoping it will pass, not fine.
The walk test If you're unsure whether pain warrants a rest day, try 10 minutes of walking. If pain increases or is clearly present while walking, rest. If it decreases, easy running is probably safe. It's a simple but often informative first step.

Which of these pain types is the most serious warning signal?
Exactly. Muscle soreness on both sides after training is DOMS, a normal part of adaptation. Discomfort at the start of a run that clears within the first few minutes is often normal. But pain that intensifies during a run and is fixed at one precise point signals a possible overload of a tendon, ligament or bone.
