Running glossary

The key running terms, explained simply: from lactate to VO₂max. The glossary is also available from the top menu in every lesson.

Aerobic
With oxygen. Aerobic training uses oxygen to generate energy. Easy and moderate running is aerobic.
Anaerobic
Without oxygen. Very intense running where oxygen is insufficient, so energy is produced anaerobically, with lactate accumulating.
Cadence
Steps per minute (SPM). The optimal range for most runners is 170–180 SPM. Lower cadence means a longer stride and greater impact force.
Capillaries
The smallest blood vessels, delivering oxygen directly to muscle fibres. Endurance training increases capillary density, one of the key mechanisms of aerobic progress.
Cardiac drift
Heart rate creep: heart rate rises while maintaining the same pace over time, especially in heat or when dehydrated.
Core
The stabilising muscles of the trunk, transverse abdominals, obliques, spinal extensors, diaphragm. Important for transferring force between the legs and arms while running.
DOMS
Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness, muscle soreness 24–48 hours after training, caused by micro-tears in muscle fibres. Not related to lactate.
Eccentric strength
A muscle's ability to control movement while lengthening (e.g. descending stairs or running downhill). Important for injury prevention.
FatMax
The intensity zone at which the body burns the most fat in absolute terms. Typically corresponds to Z2 (60–70% MaxHR).
Glycogen
Glucose stores in muscles and the liver. The main source of fast energy. Limited capacity, ~1,500–2,000 kcal, enough for ~90 min of intense running.
Lactate
A compound produced when glucose is broken down quickly. Not directly responsible for the burning sensation. That's caused by hydrogen ions. Lactate itself can be used as fuel.
Lactate threshold
The intensity level beyond which lactate in the blood accumulates faster than it is cleared. A trained runner reaches this threshold later (at a higher pace).
MaxHR
Maximum heart rate, the fastest your heart can beat. Rough formula: 220 − age. More accurately measured in a lab.
Mitochondria
The cell's "power plant", the organelle where energy is produced aerobically. Endurance training increases both the number and efficiency of mitochondria.
Overstriding
A stride in which the foot lands too far in front of the body's centre of mass. Increases impact force and the risk of knee injuries.
Pace
Running speed expressed as time, minutes per kilometre (min/km) or minutes per mile. The larger the number, the slower the pace. For example, 6:00 min/km is slower than 5:00 min/km.
Polarised model
The 80/20 training principle: 80% very easy, 20% very hard. The middle zone is avoided. A scientifically backed model used by elite athletes.
RPE
Rate of Perceived Exertion, a subjective effort scale from 1 to 10. A simple way to measure intensity without a heart-rate monitor.
Split
The time for an individual kilometre or segment of a race. A positive split means the first half was faster than the second. A negative split means the second half was faster, and usually produces the best finish time.
Supercompensation
The body's adaptation mechanism: after stress (training) and rest, the body becomes slightly more capable than before. This is the foundation of progress.
Threshold (LT)
See Lactate threshold.
VDOT
A running fitness metric developed by Jack Daniels, calculated from race results. Used to set precise training zones.
VO₂ Max
The maximum amount of oxygen the body can consume per minute. One of the key indicators of endurance capacity. Rises with aerobic training.
VT1
First ventilatory threshold: breathing rate starts to rise more quickly. Roughly corresponds to the Z2/Z3 boundary. Above it, conversation becomes harder.
VT2
Second ventilatory threshold: breathing is very intense, speech impossible. Roughly corresponds to the lactate threshold.
Z1–Z5
Training zones based on percentage of MaxHR. Z1 (50–60%): very easy. Z2 (60–70%): aerobic base. Z3 (70–80%): grey zone. Z4 (80–90%): threshold. Z5 (90–100%): maximum.
