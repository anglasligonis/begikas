Why do beginners need to run slowly?

Many beginners try to run too fast from day one. Understandable — but physiologically it's a mistake. Here's why.

Key idea: 80% of your running should be easy enough that you can hold a conversation. That's not laziness — it's a sports-science-backed principle.

Your muscles have two types of fibres: slow-twitch (Type I) and fast-twitch (Type II). Slow fibres are efficient — they use oxygen and fat as fuel and can work for hours. Fast fibres are powerful, but fatigue quickly.

When you run easily, almost only slow fibres work. When you speed up, the body recruits fast-twitch fibres and lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared . Running becomes hard not because you're weak, but because more and more work falls on the fast-twitch fibres.

Speed is built through slow training. 80% of your sessions should be easy — easy enough to hold a conversation. That's not laziness; it's a principle confirmed by sports science.

The key point Talk Test: if you can chat in full sentences while running, your pace is right. If only short replies — you're going too fast.

Common myth "No pain, no gain." Not true. Easy running builds your capillary network, trains your muscles to burn fat, and strengthens tendons. It's the foundation on which all speed is built.
For beginners If continuous running is still difficult, use the run-walk method: alternate 1–2 minutes of running with 1 minute of walking. This lets you move for longer, reduces tendon stress, and gradually builds endurance. Many successful runners started exactly this way.
What share of training should be easy, conversational runs?
Yes — about 80% of training should be easy. This builds your aerobic base and allows the body to recover between harder sessions.

Heart, pulse and training zones

Pulse rate is the simplest indicator of training intensity. But how do you interpret it correctly?

Key idea: Training zones help you run right. 80% of running should be Z1–Z2. Avoid the so-called grey Z3 zone: it's neither easy enough for adaptation nor hard enough for progress.

Your heart is a pump. Every time a muscle needs more oxygen, it beats faster. Maximum heart rate (MaxHR) is the highest rate your heart can reach. It's most accurately measured in a lab, but there's a simple approximation: 220 − age.

Training zones are defined by percentages of MaxHR. The two most important ones: Zone 2 (60–70% MaxHR) — easy, conversational pace where aerobic adaptation happens; and Zone 4 (85–95% MaxHR) — the lactate threshold , where running becomes truly hard.

A common beginner mistake is spending most training in Zone 3 (70–85%): it's too hard for easy adaptation and too easy for a meaningful training stimulus. This is known as the "grey zone trap".

How to calculate If you're 35: MaxHR ≈ 220 − 35 = 185 BPM. Zone 2 = 111–130 BPM. Zone 4 = 157–176 BPM. Important: the formula has a ±10–12 BPM margin of error and can be significantly off for some people. If your monitor shows Z2 but running feels hard, trust how you feel, not the numbers.

How does the body fuel running?

Your body has two main fuel sources. Which one is used depends on your pace — and this determines everything from weight loss to marathon finish times.

Key idea: Fat is a nearly inexhaustible source, but slow. Glycogen is fast, but limited. Slow training teaches the body to conserve glycogen.

Fat is a nearly inexhaustible energy source. Even a lean 70 kg person carries ~80,000–100,000 kcal in body fat. But there's an important limit: even a well-trained athlete's body can burn fat no faster than ~0.5–1 g per minute. At high intensity, fat delivers energy too slowly — glycogen becomes unavoidable.

Glycogen — sugar stored in muscles and the liver — is more quickly available, but limited: roughly 1,500–2,000 kcal (enough for ~90 minutes of intense running). When it runs out, the body experiences what marathon runners call the wall — a sudden onset of fatigue around the 30–35 km mark.

By training slowly, you teach the body to burn fat more efficiently and conserve glycogen for longer distances. This is one of the most important physiological adaptations — more in .

The marathon wall Around the 32–35 km mark, many marathon runners hit a sudden slowdown. The cause: depleted glycogen. The fix: train slowly for longer (building fat efficiency) and take energy gels from the 45-minute mark.
Nutrition around training Before (1–3 hrs): an easily digestible carbohydrate source (banana, oats, bread). Avoid heavy, fatty foods. After a longer or hard session (within ~1 hr): carbohydrates to replenish glycogen and protein for muscle recovery (e.g. cottage cheese with fruit, yogurt, a sandwich). After an easy short run, no special nutrition is needed — a normal meal is fine.
Why does the marathon wall occur?
Yes. Glycogen stores are limited (~90 min of intense running). When they run out, the body is forced to rely on slow fat oxidation — causing the sudden slowdown. The fix: train fat burning and consume carbohydrates during the run.

Lactate — not the enemy, but a signal

Lactate has a bad reputation. "Lactic acid will burn your muscles!" In reality, that's not quite true. Here's what actually happens.

Key idea: Lactate is not the enemy — it's a fuel. The burning sensation is caused by hydrogen ions, not lactate. The lactate threshold rises with training.

When a muscle is short of oxygen, it breaks down glucose and produces lactate. This is normal and happens constantly. The problem lies not in the lactate itself, but in the hydrogen ions (H⁺) produced alongside it, which acidify the muscle environment. That's what causes the burning sensation and fatigue.

Lactate is itself an energy source — the heart burns it, and other muscles take it up and use it. It only accumulates when it's produced faster than it can be cleared.

The lactate threshold — the intensity (see ) beyond which lactate begins to accumulate exponentially. An experienced runner reaches this threshold much later (at a higher pace) than an untrained one. The goal of training is to push this threshold as high as possible.

What does "threshold HR" mean? It's the point — as a percentage of your maximum heart rate — beyond which lactate accumulates faster than it's cleared. For example, if your MaxHR is 190 and your threshold is 75%, that means around ~143 BPM running will become noticeably harder. This threshold rises with training — so as you move the upper slider, you'll see the curve shift to the right.

Common myth "Next-day muscle soreness is caused by lactic acid." Not true. Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) comes from micro-tears in the muscle fibres — not from lactate. Lactate clears from the blood within a few hours of finishing a run.
What actually causes the burning sensation in your muscles?
Exactly. Lactate is actually a useful fuel. The burning is caused by the hydrogen ions produced alongside it, which lower muscle pH. Next-day soreness (DOMS) is micro-tears — not lactate.

Breathing while running: nose or mouth?

Breathing is the primary way to supply muscles with oxygen. How you breathe matters more than most people think.

Key idea: Easy pace — breathe through your nose. Hard pace — breathe through your mouth. Breathing is driven by CO₂, not by a lack of oxygen.

Breathing is not controlled by a lack of oxygen. It's controlled by CO₂ levels in the blood. When CO₂ builds up, the brain signals you to inhale. That's why, once you learn to tolerate slightly more CO₂, you breathe more calmly and efficiently.

Nasal breathing — slower, but higher quality. The nose warms and humidifies the air, produces nitric oxide (which dilates the pulmonary blood vessels), and encourages diaphragmatic breathing. For easy and moderate pace — it's the best option.

When intensity exceeds ~70% MaxHR, nasal breathing can no longer meet the demand for air. At that point the mouth becomes essential, and that's completely normal. Don't try to breathe through your nose during interval sessions — it hinders rather than helps.

It's important to learn to breathe with the diaphragm (belly breathing), not the chest. This draws more air with each breath.

Heat, sweating and hydration

Running slows in summer. That's a normal physiological response. Here's why — and what to do about it.

Key idea: Above 25°C, slow down and drink every 15 minutes. Even 2% dehydration significantly reduces performance.

Running generates a lot of heat — muscles are only about 25% efficient, and the remaining 75% becomes heat. It must be removed to keep body temperature from straying too far from 37°C.

The body does this mainly through sweating. But to do so, blood is redirected to the skin — the same blood that muscles would otherwise need. On a hot day, the heart must serve two demands at once, so heart rate rises even at the same pace — this is known as cardiac drift.

That's why, at the same pace, heart rate on a hot day can be 10–20 BPM higher than on a cool one. It means that on a hot day you need to slow down to stay in the same training zone.

Even 2% dehydration (1.4 kg for a 70 kg person) significantly reduces aerobic capacity. Drink before you run; while running, don't wait until you feel thirsty.

Why do you need to slow down on a hot day?
Yes. The heart must supply both muscles and skin (for cooling), so the same pace requires a higher heart rate. To stay in the same training zone, you need to slow down.

Running technique and injury prevention

Injuries are the main reason beginners give up running. Most of them can be prevented by changing a few simple things.

Key idea: Increase your cadence to 170–180 SPM. It's one of the simplest ways to reduce knee load and injury risk.

Running technique doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be safe and efficient. There's no single correct running style: elite runners look very different from each other. But there are a few common mistakes that cause most beginner injuries.

Overstriding — the most common mistake. It happens when the foot lands far in front of the body's centre of mass and acts as a brake. Every such stride sends impact force straight to the knee joint. The fix: shorten your stride and increase your cadence (steps per minute) to 170–180 SPM.

Arm swing crossing the midline — when your arms swing inward rather than straight forward and back, the body compensates by rotating. This wastes energy and loads the lower back muscles. Keep your elbows at ~90°, wrists relaxed — arm swing should go straight forward.

Heavy heel strike — not everyone condemns a heel landing, but if you can clearly hear the impact as your foot lands, your stride is probably too long. Try to land your foot under your body, not in front of it.

Trunk upright, lean slight — from the ankles, ~5–10° forward — lets gravity help you move forward. Those who stand completely upright often brake themselves with every step.

Stride technique — animated
Most common beginner injuries IT band syndrome (ITBS) — pain on the outside of the knee, often from increasing mileage too quickly. Shin splints — pain along the shinbone, often from hard surfaces and rapid volume increases. Plantar fasciitis — heel pain, often from weak foot muscles and poor shoes. For all three: reduce volume, strengthen weak areas, switch to a softer surface. Strength training helps prevent many of these injuries — see .

Practical tip To increase cadence: play music at 170–180 BPM and try to match your footfall to the beat. Or run with a metronome app. Increase by 5 SPM per week — a sudden change can cause shin pain.
Warm-up and cool-down Before running: 5–10 min easy walking or slow jogging + dynamic exercises (leg swings, squats). This prepares muscles and joints and reduces injury risk. After running: 5 min slow walking to let your heart rate drop gradually. Save static stretching for later — it's unnecessary before a run and can even be counterproductive.
About shoes For beginners, the most important thing is comfortable, correctly sized running shoes (leave ~1 cm of space at the toes). You don't need to buy the most expensive or carbon-plated shoes. What is the simplest way to reduce impact force on the knee?
Correct. A higher cadence (170–180 SPM) with a shorter stride means your foot lands under your body, not in front of it — reducing braking force and knee load. Increase cadence gradually, ~5 SPM per week.

How does a runner actually improve?

Speed increases not through willpower, but through specific biological adaptations. These only happen when you train correctly.

Every time you run, you put your body under stress. Afterwards, as it recovers, the body becomes slightly stronger — this is called supercompensation. But only when the stress was appropriate and the rest sufficient.

Specific adaptations that make you faster:

Heart: the volume of the left ventricle increases — with each beat the heart pumps more blood. After months of training, resting heart rate drops.

Muscles: mitochondria (the cell's energy factories) multiply, the capillary network becomes denser, and muscles learn to use more oxygen per minute.

Tendons and bones: strengthen more slowly than muscles — this is the most common cause of injury in beginners who increase volume too quickly.

The most important principle — the 10% rule: don't increase weekly mileage by more than 10%. It's not an absolute rule: for some, especially beginners, 5% is safer. Muscles adapt in 2–3 weeks. Bones and tendons — in 3–6 months. If your body sends warning signals earlier, listen to it, not the calendar.

Sleep and rest — not a luxury, but part of training. Adaptation happens not during the run, but during sleep, when the body repairs muscle fibres, produces growth hormone, and consolidates neuromotor skills. Fewer than 7 hours of sleep consistently slows progress and increases injury risk.

Beginner plan (summary) 80% of sessions — easy (you can talk). 15% — moderate (tempo). 5% — intense (intervals). Increase mileage no faster than ~10% per week. After every hard session — an easy day or rest. Sleep 7–9 hrs.

When does physical adaptation (getting stronger) actually happen?
Yes. Training is only the stimulus — the actual strengthening (supercompensation) happens during recovery, especially sleep. That's why rest and 7–9 hrs of sleep are just as important a part of training as running itself.

Training systems

The world's best runners train very differently. What actually works — and what can you borrow from them?

Key idea: All systems share one trait: 70–80% of training is easy. The methodologies differ; the principle does not.

There is no single "correct" training system. But there are several well-researched systems used by elite runners, grounded in solid physiological logic. Each has its strengths, limitations, and the contexts where it works best.

It's important to understand: even the best training systems are principles, not recipes. A beginner doesn't need to copy Jakob Ingebrigtsen's training programme — but they can understand why it works and borrow the core logic.

Norwegian double-threshold system
Gjert Ingebrigtsen · Jakob Ingebrigtsen, Filip Ingebrigtsen, Gustav Iden
High volume Not for beginners 5K–marathon Coach supervision required
Principle: two threshold sessions per day — morning and evening — with easy running in between. Intensity is precisely controlled via lactate tests: each threshold session ends at ~2–2.5 mmol/L, never exceeding the threshold. High-intensity intervals (VO₂ Max) are barely used.

The logic is simple: enormous threshold volume → the threshold rises → you can run faster before lactate accumulates. The Norwegian method requires an excellent aerobic base and continuous lactate monitoring — without these, it doesn't work.

Kenyan / Ethiopian model — massive volume, run slowly
Eliud Kipchoge, Kenenisa Bekele, Patrick Sang (coach)

Principle: incredibly high volume (160–220 km per week), mostly slow, at altitude. In Iten (Kenya, ~2400 m) and Bekoji (Ethiopia, ~3000 m), children run to school and work from an early age — the aerobic base is built over decades before serious competition.

Speed develops not through frequent intense training, but through an extraordinary aerobic base and altitude adaptation. Group running helps maintain pace and motivation even in very easy sessions.

Polarised model (80/20)
Stephen Seiler · Research-backed model
Research-backed Moderate–high volume All distances Suitable for amateurs
A model of elite athletes researched by Stephen Seiler: 80% very easy training, 20% very hard. The middle zone (Z3) is deliberately avoided — that "comfortable-hard" zone where most amateurs spend most of their training is neither easy enough for aerobic base-building nor hard enough for higher-intensity adaptation.

Research with elite swimmers, cyclists and runners consistently shows that the polarised model outperforms traditional "moderate intensity everywhere". Highly suited to amateurs, as it reduces injury risk and allows long-term training without overtraining.

Lydiard system — aerobic base
Arthur Lydiard · New Zealand · 1950s–60s
Historically influential High volume Marathon / half marathon Long base-building
The system developed by Arthur Lydiard: first, high volume (160+ km per week) at a purely aerobic pace — months without intensive intervals. Only then: hill running, intervals, speed work. The system produced several Olympic champions (Peter Snell, Murray Halberg) and fundamentally changed the understanding of running training worldwide.

The core idea: without a solid aerobic base, no speed work will reach its full potential. Lydiard's influence is felt in every modern training system — the polarised model, the Norwegian school and Kenyan practice.

Very popular among amateurs for its specificity — you know exactly what pace to run in every session. The system automatically recalibrates as performance improves. A good choice for those who prefer structure and a clear system.

Strength training for runners

Running alone doesn't protect against injuries. Strength work is not a supplementary activity — it's an essential part of a running programme.

Key idea: 2 strength sessions per week reduce injury risk, improve running economy and let you run faster — even without adding kilometres.

Running is a single-leg movement — with every stride, the entire body weight falls on one foot. This demands considerable hip, gluteal and core stability strength. When these muscles are too weak, impact force is redistributed to the knees, shins and ankles — where most running injuries begin.

Runners don't aim for muscle mass with strength training. The goal is stability, eccentric strength and tendon resilience. Research shows that a 6–8 week strength programme improves running economy (less energy for the same pace) by an average of 2–8%.

Most importantly: start strength work before pain appears — as prevention, not treatment. Muscles respond in 4–6 weeks, tendons in 3–6 months.

Why does strength training improve running economy?
Exactly. Strength work improves tendon elasticity and the function of stabilising muscles — meaning less energy is wasted on compensatory movements and the body transfers force to the ground more efficiently.
